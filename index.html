Abstract

               This article explores how the staged combustion cycle used in the RS-25 engines helped develop 
               the Space Shuttle into a partially reusable rocket-launched vehicle. Using a staged combustion 
               cycle allowed the Space Shuttle engines to effectively use 62,300 gallons of fuel per minute during
               its ascent (Space Shuttle Propulsion Systems, 2005). Staged combustion cycles are advantageous as
               they use all their propellants in the main combustion chamber. This design allows for high fuel 
               efficiency and higher thrust levels of 1.2 million pounds of thrust during
               liftoff (History of the Space Shuttle Main Engines, no date).

Introduction

              The RS-25 is a liquid-fuel cryogenic rocket engine that burns liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen as propellants.
              It was built in the United States by Rocketdyne, with development beginning around the 1970s (Terry, 2025).
              The Space Shuttle utilised a cluster of three RS-25 engines, which were used for propulsion during ascent. A 
              staged combustion cycle is a combustion technique in which the fuel and oxidiser are partially burned in separate 
              stages before being combined and ignited in the main combustion chamber. While early rockets relied on gas generator
              cycles, the Space Shuttle utilised a staged combustion cycle. Traditional gas generator cycles offered reliability 
              but suffered from incomplete fuel combustion and lower thrust levels. It was decided that the RS-25 engines would
              use staged combustion rather than gas generator cycles due to the higher thrust levels of 2279kN compared to 1000kN
              of a typical gas generator cycle. Staged combustion cycle engines are also reusable, and designed for 100s of uses
              after inspection and refurbishment, which highly suited the Space Shuttle's needs.
